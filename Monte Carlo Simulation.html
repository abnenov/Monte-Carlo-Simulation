=== MONTE CARLO SIMULATION ===
What is a simulation?
- Simulation is a technique for modeling real systems using computer programs
- Used in science to study complex systems that are difficult to solve analytically
- Useful because it allows testing different scenarios without real experiments

How are statistics useful in simulation?
- We use random numbers to model uncertain processes
- We apply probability theory to analyze results
- Through multiple iterations we obtain statistically valid results

What is a Monte Carlo simulation?
- Method for solving deterministic problems through random sampling
- Named after the gambling district in Monaco
- Especially useful for multidimensional integrals and complex probability problems

=== PERFORMANCE COMPARISON ===
Method         | Accuracy | Time    | Advantages        | Disadvantages
---------------------------------------------------------------------------
Monte Carlo    | Medium   | Fast    | Universal, simple | Stochastic
Trapezoidal    | High     | Medium  | Deterministic     | 1D only, complex
Analytical     | Exact    | Fastest | Exact result      | Not always possible

Monte Carlo is especially useful when:
- We have multidimensional integrals
- Integration domain is complex
- Analytical solution is difficult or impossible
- We seek approximation rather than exact result

=== CONCLUSION ===
Monte Carlo simulation is a powerful technique that:
1. Uses randomness to solve deterministic problems
2. Is especially useful for complex, multidimensional tasks
3. Offers a compromise between accuracy and implementation simplicity
4. Finds wide application in various fields of science
5. Demonstrates the importance of statistics in computer science

Key applications demonstrated:
- Numerical integration
- Mathematical constant estimation (π)
- Financial modeling (option pricing)
- Geometric area calculation

Advantages:
- Easy to implement and understand
- Scales well to high dimensions
- Parallelizable
- Handles complex geometries

Disadvantages:
- Convergence can be slow (O(1/√n))
- Results have inherent randomness
- May require many samples for high precision
